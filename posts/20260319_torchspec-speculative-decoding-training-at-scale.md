# TorchSpec: Speculative Decoding Training at Scale

- Authors: TorchSpec team, Mooncake team
- Date: March 19, 2026
- Source: https://pytorch.org/blog/torchspec-speculative-decoding-training-at-scale/

## Introduction

Over the past year, large language models have rapidly expanded in both scale and capability. Frontier models such as Kimi K2.5, GLM 5, and Qwen 3.5 now operate with hundreds of billions of parameters and context windows stretching to millions of tokens, enabling long-context reasoning, agentic workflows, and complex tool use. As these models grow more capable, efficient inference has become one of the most critical systems challenges in LLM deployment.

Speculative decoding is one of the most effective techniques for accelerating LLM generation. With speculative decoding, a lightweight draft model proposes several tokens ahead, while a larger target model verifies them in a single forward pass. When predictions are accepted, multiple tokens can be generated at once, improving throughput and latency. Recent approaches such as MTP (Multi Token Prediction) and [EAGLE-3](https://arxiv.org/abs/2503.01840) demonstrate that well-trained draft models can deliver consistent acceleration.

An important aspect of draft model training is transferring information from the target model to the draft model through intermediate hidden states. As frontier LLMs get larger, a new bottleneck appears: efficiently passing the massive volume of hidden states generated by the target model to the draft model. For example, EAGLE-3 relies on 3 layers of target-model hidden states. When training an EAGLE-3 draft model for Kimi K2.5, a single 128K-token training sample requires about 7 GB of hidden states from the target model.

Existing pipelines typically follow one of two approaches. One option is to precompute hidden states and store them on disk, which leads to massive storage requirements and severe I/O pressure. Another option is to co-locate inference and training to generate hidden states on the fly during draft model training, avoiding disk materialization but introducing significant GPU memory pressure.

To address these challenges, the team introduces [TorchSpec](https://github.com/torchspec-project/TorchSpec), a torch-native framework for disaggregated speculative decoding training. TorchSpec separates the inference system that generates hidden states from the training system that consumes them. Instead of writing hidden states to disk, they are streamed directly from inference engine workers to training workers through [Mooncake](https://github.com/kvcache-ai/Mooncake) using RDMA (Remote Direct Memory Access) or TCP.

With TorchSpec, the team reports training a [Kimi K2.5 EAGLE-3 draft model](https://huggingface.co/lightseekorg/kimi-k2.5-eagle3) with 1500 H200 GPU hours, scaling to [600k training samples](https://huggingface.co/datasets/lightseekorg/kimi-mtp-dataset) (6 billion tokens).

With the trained draft model, output throughput improves by over +60% at batch size 1, +30% at batch size 8, and +26% at batch size 16 under a lookahead of 3 tokens.

## Background

There are two common approaches to speculative decoding training today:

- Inference co-located training
- Offline hidden states preparation

Each works at moderate scale but struggles as draft model size and context lengths grow.

### Inference Co-located Training

In co-located training, the target model and draft model share the same GPUs. The target model runs a forward pass to produce hidden states and logits, which are immediately consumed by the draft model for training. This introduces several constraints:

- Rigid sharding: The draft model parallelism strategy is tied to the target model. If the target model uses TP=4, the draft model must also use 4 ranks even if another setting would be better.
- Training and inference are not independently scalable: Co-located frameworks often lack cross-node sharding support, limiting training to GPUs within one node and coupling resource counts.
- Memory pressure: The target model occupies a large portion of GPU memory, leaving limited memory for draft model training.

Memory analysis for co-located training with [Kimi K2.5](https://huggingface.co/moonshotai/Kimi-K2.5) (1T parameter MoE, 384 experts, ~575 GB model weight):

| GPU | Total Memory (8 GPUs) | Model Weight | Per-GPU Shard | Remaining per GPU |
| --- | --- | --- | --- | --- |
| 8xH200 | 1,128 GB | ~575 GB | ~72 GB | ~69 GB |
| 8xH100 | 640 GB | ~575 GB | ~72 GB | ~8 GB |

*Although the draft model is typically small, state-of-the-art methods like Training-Time Testing (TTT) retain intermediate activations for multiple speculative steps, increasing memory usage. With 8 GB of memory, training can be limited to context length 4096.*

### Offline Hidden States Preparation

The offline approach precomputes hidden states from the target model, serializes them to disk, and loads them later for draft model training. This decouples inference from training, but creates major storage pressure for large models and long contexts.

*Storage analysis for Kimi K2.5 (hidden_size=7168, vocab_size=163,840):*

*Per sample at context length = 131,072 tokens:*

| Tensor | Shape | Dtype | Size |
| --- | --- | --- | --- |
| Hidden states (3 aux layers) | (131072, 21504) | bf16 | 5.25 GB |
| Last hidden states | (131072, 7168) | bf16 | 1.75 GB |
| Input IDs | (131072,) | int64 | 1 MB |
| Total per sample |  |  | ~7.0 GB |

*Note: target logits can be recomputed from last hidden states via lm_head, so they do not need to be stored.*

| Dataset Size | Storage Required |
| --- | --- |
| 10,000 samples | 70 TB |
| 30,000 samples | 210 TB |
| 100,000 samples | 700 TB |

At this scale, distributed file systems face heavy pressure, especially when multiple speculative training runs happen concurrently and compete for I/O bandwidth. Serialization and deserialization overhead can also slow training.

## TorchSpec: Disaggregated Draft Model Training

TorchSpec takes a different approach: fully disaggregated inference and training. The target model runs on dedicated inference GPUs, the draft model trains on separate training GPUs, and tensor data is streamed between them through RDMA or TCP via the [Mooncake](https://github.com/kvcache-ai/Mooncake) store.

This architecture addresses key challenges:

1. Flexible, independent scaling: Inference and training GPU counts are independent, enabling better throughput tuning.
2. Full memory for training: Training GPUs are dedicated to the draft model, maximizing memory for long sequences and large batches.
3. No storage overhead: Hidden states stream directly from inference to training without disk offload.

![Architecture figure from source page](https://pytorch.org/wp-content/uploads/2026/03/unnamed-4.png)

### Why Mooncake?

Originally developed by Moonshot AI and Tsinghua University, Mooncake is a transfer engine for KV cache management in production LLM serving. It now has broad community adoption in the PyTorch ecosystem. Mooncake provides high-throughput cross-node transfer across network protocols and manages memory lifecycle, matching TorchSpec's hidden-state streaming needs.

Key properties:

- RDMA + TCP with a unified API: Near-line-rate transfers on InfiniBand/RoCE and fallback to TCP without code changes.
- GPU Direct RDMA: Direct transfer into GPU memory without CPU staging.
- Zero-copy transfers: Tensors are packed in pre-registered pinned-memory buffers and transferred without serialization or extra copies.
- Production-grade reliability: Proven in large-scale production settings.

### Long Context Support

With memory fully dedicated to the draft model, TorchSpec supports sequence lengths that are difficult to reach in co-located EAGLE-3 training. The blog notes that Kimi K2.5 consumes 72 GB in co-located mode; with lookahead=4 and disaggregated training, a single H100 can train up to 44K tokens and a single B200 can scale to 200K tokens.

![Long-context figure from source page](https://pytorch.org/wp-content/uploads/2026/03/unnamed-5.png)

Beyond disaggregation, TorchSpec uses an inference-engine-native implementation where hidden states are generated directly by production inference engines. Benefits include:

- Inference-training alignment: Template formatting, tokenization, and kernels stay aligned across training and deployment.
- Native model support through the engine: New target model architectures require minimal training-side changes.

Currently, TorchSpec supports vLLM and SGLang, with TensorRT LLM support planned. If an inference engine supports a model, TorchSpec can typically train a draft model for it, including:

- New model architectures (MoE, multi-modal, etc.)
- Quantized models (FP8, INT4, etc.)
- Sparse attention, RoPE variants, and other model-specific features

### Train with Decode

Draft models often perform best when trained on the target model token distribution. A common approach is to keep original prompts and regenerate responses with the target model in a separate preprocessing stage. TorchSpec's engine-native design allows autoregressive output generation from prompt-only inputs during training, simplifying this workflow.

### Case Study: Training an EAGLE-3 Model for Kimi K2.5

Kimi K2.5 provides a challenging scenario that illustrates the disaggregated approach.

#### The Challenge

- Model scale: Kimi K2.5 requires at least 8xH200 or 16xH100 GPUs just to serve the target model, leaving little memory for co-located draft training.
- Long context: Workloads require training on sequences up to 200,000 tokens.
- Large vocabulary: 163,840-token vocabulary with hidden dimension 7,168.

### The TorchSpec Solution

TorchSpec recommends deploying Kimi K2.5 on dedicated 8xH200 inference GPUs and training the EAGLE-3 draft model on another 8xH200 GPUs. This separates serving and training memory budgets and enables long-context training at 100,000 tokens with 600k data samples.

**Scripts**

- 3 nodes of 8xH100 with TP=16 inference and TP=8 training: [kimi-k25-3node-h100](https://github.com/torchspec-project/TorchSpec/tree/main/examples/kimi-k25-3node-h100)
- 2 nodes of 8xH200 with TP=8 inference and TP=8 training: [kimi-k25-2node-h200](https://github.com/torchspec-project/TorchSpec/tree/main/examples/kimi-k25-2node-h200)

**Training Dataset**: [kimi-600k-training-dataset](https://huggingface.co/datasets/lightseekorg/kimi-mtp-dataset)

**Draft Model**: [kimi-k2.5-eagle3](https://huggingface.co/lightseekorg/kimi-k2.5-eagle3)

## Roadmap

TorchSpec is under active development. Key areas include:

- Improve model coverage: Support for Minimax M2.5, Qwen 3.5, and continuous training of the MTP layer from GLM 5.
- Packed sequence training: Pack shorter sequences into single samples to improve GPU utilization and reduce padding waste.
- Additional training algorithms: Expand beyond EAGLE-3 to include approaches like DFlash and MTP.
- Engine integrations: Integrate additional inference engines, such as TensorRT LLM.
